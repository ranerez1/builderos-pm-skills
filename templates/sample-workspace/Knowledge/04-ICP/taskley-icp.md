# Taskley – Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

## Primary ICP: Overwhelmed solo knowledge worker

### Summary
Taskley’s primary ICP is an **individual knowledge worker** in their mid‑20s to early‑40s who juggles multiple responsibilities and feels that their current task tools help them capture items but not actually *do* them.
They are willing to pay a reasonable subscription if the tool gives them a daily sense of control and progress.

### Demographics
- Age: 25–42.
- Location: Primarily North America and Western Europe, living in mid‑ to large‑size cities.
- Employment: Full‑time knowledge workers (product, marketing, consulting, engineering, design, operations), freelancers, and graduate students.
- Income: Comfortable but not high‑end; subscription sensitivity exists, but productivity is seen as a justifiable expense.

### Psychographics and attitudes
- Feels like their life is a mix of work tasks, side projects, and personal errands that blur together.
- Has tried multiple task or note apps in the past, often "churning" because none stuck.
- Values structure and clarity, but resists heavy processes or systems that feel like work.
- Frequently says things like:
  - "I know what I should do, I just don’t plan my time well."
  - "I’m great at starting things, not so great at finishing."
  - "My calendar is full of meetings, but my real work lives in random lists."

### Behaviors
- Uses their phone as the primary capture device; desktop mainly for planning.
- Already uses a digital calendar (Google/Apple/Outlook) and 1–2 communication tools (Gmail, Slack, WhatsApp, etc.).
- Currently uses generic to‑do apps, notes apps, or a mix of paper and digital.
- Plans sporadically: some days they plan, many days they "wing it".
- Has a backlog of overdue tasks and past‑due reminders they mostly ignore.

### Jobs to be done
When life feels chaotic and everything feels urgent, Taskley’s primary user wants to:
- Turn all their scattered tasks (apps, emails, notes, sticky notes) into a single, trustworthy list.
- Decide what is realistically achievable today versus what can wait.
- Coordinate work and personal commitments in one place without overscheduling.
- Feel a sense of accomplishment at the end of the day, even if everything wasn’t finished.

Functional jobs:
- Capture tasks quickly across devices.
- See a realistic view of today that includes time, energy, and priorities.
- Adjust the plan when meetings move or unexpected work appears.
- Review progress and refine habits over time.

Emotional jobs:
- Reduce the anxiety and guilt of "I should have done more".
- Feel like a reliable, organized person to themselves and others.
- Build confidence that they can handle their workload over time, not just in sprints.

### Pain points with current tools
- Traditional to‑do apps become infinite backlogs with little guidance on what to do next.
- Calendar tools are good for meetings, but bad at representing deep work and small tasks.
- Apps that try to do "everything" feel bloated and overwhelming.
- Manual priority and due‑date management is tedious; everything ends up being "urgent".
- Existing tools often assume a stable workday, whereas reality is meetings, interruptions, and context switching.

### Why they choose Taskley
- Taskley emphasizes **daily planning and trade‑offs** rather than infinite lists.
- The Today Plan helps them commit to a realistic subset of work.
- Energy and timeboxing features align with how their day actually feels, not just what’s on the calendar.
- Rituals and gentle nudges help them build a lightweight routine without a heavy productivity system.

## Secondary ICPs (for context only)

### 1. Ambitious students and early‑career professionals
- Use case: Balancing classes, part‑time work, personal projects, and social life.
- Motivation: Avoid missing deadlines, manage study blocks, and reduce last‑minute cramming.
- Behavior: Heavy mobile usage, responsive to streaks, badges, and gamified progress.

### 2. Solo creators and side‑project builders
- Use case: Managing a day job plus content creation, side businesses, or learning new skills.
- Motivation: Make consistent, small progress on long‑term goals.
- Behavior: Often work in irregular bursts, evenings and weekends; appreciate tools that help them protect time for deep work.

For the workshop exercises, assume you are primarily optimizing the experience for the **overwhelmed solo knowledge worker**, while keeping the secondary ICPs in mind to avoid breaking their workflows.
